scFM BenchScope

scFM BenchScope is an agentic evaluation framework for single-cell foundation models (scFMs), designed to assess both representation quality and clinical-task utility. It benchmarks models across biological conservation, batch mixing, and classification performance using diverse cancer datasets and clinically relevant prediction tasks. The framework also supports automated discovery, classification, and summarization of emerging scFMs, enabling continuous evaluation of new models for applications such as patient outcome prediction.

Benchmarked models

The following models have been evaluated through the full scFM evaluation framework across all metric categories:

  • scGPT — Generative pre-trained transformer trained on 33 million human single-cell transcriptomes.
  • Geneformer — BERT-style transformer pre-trained on 30 million human single-cell transcriptomes with rank-value encoding of gene expression.
  • scFoundation — 100-million parameter model pre-trained on 50 million human single cells with an asymmetric transformer architecture.
  • CellPLM — Encodes cell-cell relationships beyond individual cells using a Gaussian mixture latent space.
  • Nicheformer — Joint foundation model for dissociated single-cell and spatial transcriptomics, trained on 110 million cells.
  • Scimilarity — Metric learning foundation model trained on about 22 million single-cell profiles.
  • scConcept — Contrastive pre-training approach that learns technology-agnostic single-cell representations without relying on reconstruction objectives.
  • STATE — Context-aware perturbation foundation model that predicts transcriptional responses to genetic and chemical perturbations across diverse cell types and experimental conditions.

Metric dictionary

scFM BenchScope evaluates single-cell foundation models across three complementary dimensions: biological conservation, batch mixing, and clinical-task utility. Together, these metrics assess whether model embeddings preserve meaningful cell-state structure, avoid technical batch artifacts, and support clinically relevant prediction tasks.

Biological conservation

These metrics measure whether the learned embedding preserves biologically meaningful structure, such as cell types, cell states, and neighborhood relationships.

Normalized Mutual Information

Abbreviation: NMI

Measures the agreement between unsupervised clusters and known biological labels, such as annotated cell types. Higher NMI indicates that the embedding organizes cells in a way that better reflects known biological structure.

Direction: Higher is better.

Adjusted Rand Index

Abbreviation: ARI

Measures similarity between clustering assignments and known labels while correcting for random agreement. ARI is useful for evaluating whether cell populations are separated into biologically coherent groups.

Direction: Higher is better.

Label Silhouette Score

Abbreviation: ASW Label

Measures how well cells with the same biological label group together compared with cells from different labels. A higher score indicates stronger separation between biological populations.

Direction: Higher is better.

Graph Connectivity

Evaluates whether cells from the same biological label remain connected in the nearest-neighbor graph. This is useful for assessing whether embeddings preserve continuous biological manifolds within each cell population.

Direction: Higher is better.

Cell-type Local Inverse Simpson’s Index

Abbreviation: cLISI

Measures local diversity of biological labels in the embedding neighborhood. For biological conservation, low diversity across distinct cell types can indicate that biologically different populations remain separated.

Direction: Interpretation depends on task; often lower is better when preserving distinct labels.

kNN Label F1 Score

Uses the labels of nearest neighbors to predict each cell’s biological label and reports the F1 score. Higher values indicate that local neighborhoods are enriched for cells of the same biological class.

Direction: Higher is better.

kNN Label Accuracy

Measures how accurately a cell’s label can be predicted from its nearest neighbors. This reflects how well the embedding captures local cell-type structure.

Direction: Higher is better.

kNN Label Purity

Measures the fraction of nearest neighbors that share the same biological label as the query cell. High label purity suggests that the embedding places biologically similar cells close together.

Direction: Higher is better.

Batch mixing

These metrics measure whether embeddings reduce technical variation across batches, donors, platforms, or datasets while preserving biological signal.

k-nearest-neighbor Batch Effect Test

Abbreviation: kBET

Tests whether the local batch composition around each cell matches the expected global batch composition. Better kBET performance indicates that batches are well mixed in the embedding space.

Direction: Higher acceptance rate is better.

Integration Local Inverse Simpson’s Index

Abbreviation: iLISI

Measures local batch diversity in the embedding. A higher iLISI score indicates that cells from different batches are well mixed within local neighborhoods.

Direction: Higher is better.

Batch Silhouette Score

Abbreviation: Batch ASW

Measures whether cells separate by technical batch in the embedding space. A good integrated representation should reduce batch-driven separation.

Direction: Lower raw batch separation is better; transformed scores may be reported so higher is better.

Principal Component Regression Batch Score

Abbreviation: PCR Batch

Quantifies how much variation in the embedding can be explained by batch labels. Lower batch-associated variance suggests better removal of technical effects.

Direction: Lower raw batch effect is better; transformed scores may be reported so higher is better.

kNN Batch Purity

Measures the fraction of nearest neighbors that come from the same batch as the query cell. High batch purity can indicate poor batch mixing, while lower purity suggests better integration across batches.

Direction: Lower is usually better.

Batch Prediction Accuracy

Measures how accurately batch labels can be predicted from the embedding. If batch labels are easy to predict, the embedding may still contain strong technical artifacts.

Direction: Lower is usually better.

Classification and clinical-task utility

These metrics evaluate whether scFM embeddings support downstream prediction tasks, including patient outcome prediction, treatment response classification, and other clinically relevant endpoints.

Area Under the Precision–Recall Curve

Abbreviation: AUPRC

Measures the trade-off between precision and recall across classification thresholds. AUPRC is especially useful for imbalanced clinical outcomes, where positive cases may be rare.

Direction: Higher is better.

Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve

Abbreviation: AUROC / AUC

Measures how well the classifier ranks positive examples above negative examples across thresholds. AUROC summarizes discrimination performance but may be less informative than AUPRC for highly imbalanced tasks.

Direction: Higher is better.

F1 Score

Harmonic mean of precision and recall. F1 is useful when both false positives and false negatives matter and when class imbalance makes accuracy less informative.

Direction: Higher is better.

Accuracy

Measures the fraction of correctly classified samples. Accuracy is intuitive but can be misleading when outcome classes are imbalanced.

Direction: Higher is better.

Precision

Measures the fraction of predicted positive cases that are truly positive. High precision indicates fewer false-positive predictions.

Direction: Higher is better.

Recall

Measures the fraction of true positive cases that are correctly identified. High recall indicates fewer missed positive cases.

Direction: Higher is better.
